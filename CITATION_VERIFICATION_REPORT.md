```markdown
### PATTERN CATEGORY 8B: ADVANCED REASONING STRUCTURES

#### Pattern 8C: Tree of Thoughts / Graph of Thoughts

**Background:** Tree of Thoughts (ToT) and Graph of Thoughts (GoT) represent
advances beyond Chain-of-Thought reasoning, enabling LLMs to explore multiple
solution paths simultaneously. Originally proposed by Yao et al. (2023) and
Long et al. (2023), these frameworks formalize exploratory reasoning for
complex problems [Yao et al., 2023; Long et al., 2023].

**Exact Prompt Template:**
```

I need to solve: [complex problem requiring exploration]

Think of this as exploring a tree of possibilities:

- Level 1: Generate [number] initial approaches or interpretations
- For each Level 1 option, generate [number] Level 2 refinements
- For promising branches, generate Level 3 detailed solutions
- At each step, evaluate: "Is this path promising? Why/why not?"

Finally, compare the complete solutions and choose the best path with justification.

```

**Concrete Example:**

```

Problem: Design a go-to-market strategy for an AI product in healthcare.

Tree of Thoughts exploration:
Level 1 - Positioning Approaches:

1. Position as "Productivity tool for clinicians"
2. Position as "Compliance/documentation solution"
3. Position as "Research/data analysis tool"

For each positioning (Level 2):

- Positioning 1 branch:
  - Buyer: Chief Medical Officer focus?
  - Buyer: Clinical staff focus?
  - Buyer: Health IT director focus?

For each buyer (Level 3):

- Full go-to-market strategy with channels, messaging, pricing

Evaluate which branch has best market fit, fastest sales cycle, lowest CAC.


```markdown
### Framework: Six Thinking Hats (Edward de Bono, 1985)

The Six Thinking Hats is a parallel thinking framework developed by physician
Dr. Edward de Bono in 1985 [de Bono, 1985], designed to improve group decision-making
and brainstorming by directing attention sequentially through six distinct thinking
modes [The Decision Lab, 2021]:

**The Six Hat Colors:**

1. **White Hat** - Facts, information, data-based thinking
2. **Yellow Hat** - Optimism, benefits, positive outcomes
3. **Black Hat** - Critical judgment, risks, downsides
4. **Red Hat** - Emotions, feelings, intuition, gut responses
5. **Green Hat** - Creativity, alternatives, new ideas
6. **Blue Hat** - Process control, organization, meta-thinking

**How to Apply to Brainstorming:**

Each "hat" phase focuses exclusively on one thinking mode. Participants cannot
mix perspectives - this forces comprehensive exploration [Chamblee & deSantis, 2025].

Template for brainstorming session:
```

We're evaluating [idea/proposal].

White Hat phase (5 min): What facts do we know? What data do we need?
Yellow Hat phase (5 min): What are the best outcomes if this succeeds?
Black Hat phase (5 min): What could fail? What are the risks?
Red Hat phase (3 min): What do you feel about this instinctively?
Green Hat phase (10 min): What alternatives or improvements exist?
Blue Hat phase (5 min): Summary - Where are we? Next steps?


```markdown
### Framework: TRIZ (Theory of Inventive Problem Solving)

TRIZ, an acronym for the Russian "Teoriya Resheniya Izobreta'elskikh Zadach"
(Theory of Inventive Problem Solving), is a systematic methodology developed
by Soviet engineer Genrich Altshuller beginning in 1946 [Altshuller, 1946-1998;
Wikipedia, 2025].

**Historical Foundation:**
Altshuller recognized that technological advancements follow systematic patterns
by analyzing over 40,000 patents. Rather than relying on trial-and-error
brainstorming, TRIZ provides 40 universal innovation principles that have been
successfully applied across industries [Ladewig et al., 2008].

**Core TRIZ Concepts:**

1. Technical contradictions (choosing one attribute benefits another detriment)
2. Physical contradictions (an object must have opposite properties)
3. Ideality (systems evolve toward ideal final result with no harmful effects)
4. Laws of technical system evolution (predictable development patterns)

**Applying TRIZ to LLM Brainstorming:**

When traditional brainstorming fails, use TRIZ to systematically identify
solutions [Zornes, 2014]:
```

Step 1: Define Your Specific Problem

- Clarify the technical contradiction
- Identify what attributes conflict

Step 2: Map to TRIZ General Problem Category

- Which of the 39 problem parameters are involved?
- What contradictions appear?

Step 3: Apply TRIZ Principles

- The contradiction matrix suggests which of 40 principles apply
- Generate solutions using these principles, not trial-and-error

Step 4: Apply General Solutions to Your Context

- Adapt principle-based solutions to your specific situation

